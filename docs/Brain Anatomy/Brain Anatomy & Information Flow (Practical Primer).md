## Purpose and scope

This document is a practical, anatomy-oriented primer. It describes:
- the main anatomical divisions of the human brain,
- what key structures typically do,
- and how information generally travels from “input” (sensory signals) to “output” (action, speech, autonomic responses).[^a1]

It is not a diagnostic or psychiatric guide. It also avoids oversimplified “one region = one function” claims, but keeps the presentation usable as a reference.

---

## Core neurophysiology: what a “signal” is

This section provides minimal “mechanics” that explain how the brain can implement perception, memory, and control at a physical level.

### Neurons and action potentials (the basic unit)
- A neuron is an excitable cell that can generate an electrical impulse called an **action potential**.[^n1]
- Action potentials are “all-or-none” spikes that travel along the neuron’s axon.[^n2]
- Many important brain signals are not a single spike but **patterns of spikes** across populations of neurons.[^n3]

### Synapses (communication between neurons)
- Neurons communicate at **synapses**, where one neuron influences another.[^s1]
- Most synapses are **chemical**: neurotransmitters are released and bind to receptors on the next neuron.[^s2]
- Synapses can be:
  - **Excitatory** (increasing the chance the next neuron fires),
  - **Inhibitory** (decreasing the chance it fires).

### Excitation and inhibition (E/I balance)[^ei1]
A practical minimal map:
- **Glutamate** is the primary excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain.[^ei2]
- **GABA** is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter.[^ei3]
- Many brain functions rely on maintaining a stable **excitation/inhibition balance**:
  - Too much excitation can increase instability and “noise.”[^ei4]
  - Too much inhibition can suppress responsiveness and integration.

### Plasticity (how learning changes the system)
- **Synaptic plasticity** is the ability of synapses to strengthen or weaken over time.[^pl1]
- A common concept-level framing:
  - **LTP (long-term potentiation):** strengthening of synaptic influence.[^pl2]
  - **LTD (long-term depression):** weakening of synaptic influence.[^pl3]
- Plasticity supports learning, habit formation, and memory consolidation.[^pl4]

### Glia and myelination (support and speed)
- **Glial cells** support neurons by regulating the chemical environment, energy supply, and immune-like responses.[^g1]
- **Myelin** is an insulating layer around axons that increases conduction speed and reliability.[^g2]
- White-matter “integrity” strongly affects network communication and processing speed.[^g3]

## Neuromodulators: “system modes”

Beyond fast excitation/inhibition, the brain uses **neuromodulators** that change how circuits behave globally.[^m0] They do not simply “turn one function on,” but adjust **gain**,[^m1] **learning**, **arousal**,[^m2] and **policy selection**.[^m3]

### Dopamine (DA)[^da0]
Practical summary:
- Supports reinforcement learning: updating values based on outcomes (reward prediction signals).[^da1]
- Influences action selection and habit learning via basal ganglia loops.[^da2]
- Affects motivation/effort allocation and “policy” selection (which plan to pursue).[^da3]

### Norepinephrine / Noradrenaline (NE)[^ne0]
Practical summary:
- Strongly linked to arousal, vigilance, and responsiveness to salient events.[^ne1]
- Increases signal-to-noise under certain conditions (sharper reactivity).[^ne2]
- High NE often correlates with heightened alertness and stress-related hyperreactivity.[^ne3]

### Serotonin (5-HT)[^5ht0]
Practical summary:
- Broadly modulates mood, patience/impulsivity, and affect regulation.[^5ht1]
- Influences how strongly internal state shapes perception and decision-making.[^5ht2]
- Impacts sleep–wake architecture and stress responsivity.[^5ht3]

### Acetylcholine (ACh)[^ach0]
Practical summary:
- Supports attention, sensory processing “precision,” and learning/encoding.[^ach1]
- Helps cortical circuits shift between internally driven activity and externally driven processing.[^ach2]
- Plays a role in memory encoding and certain aspects of REM-related dynamics.[^rem0]

### Additional modulators (brief)
- **Orexin/hypocretin:** stabilizes wakefulness; loss is linked to narcolepsy.[^ox0]
- **Histamine:** supports wakefulness and cortical activation.[^his0]
- **Endogenous opioids:** influence pain and affect (analgesia, stress buffering).[^opio0]

Key takeaway:
- Neuromodulators act like **global regime parameters** for the brain: they change how the same anatomical circuits behave under different states (stress, fatigue, motivation, threat).[^m4]

## Macroanatomy: the major divisions

### A. Cerebrum / cerebral hemispheres (telencephalon)[^tel0]
What most people call “the brain” in everyday language.

Main components:
- Cerebral cortex (outer gray matter): layered neural tissue supporting perception, cognition, and voluntary control.[^gmwm0]
- White matter (inner tracts): long-range axonal connections between cortical and subcortical areas.[^gmwm1]
- Subcortical nuclei (within hemispheres): e.g., basal ganglia, parts of limbic circuitry.[^sc0]

### B. Diencephalon (intermediate brain)[^di0]
A central hub between cortex and brainstem:
- Thalamus: major relay/coordination hub for sensory and cortical communication.[^thal0]
- Hypothalamus: homeostasis, endocrine regulation, autonomic control, stress and basic drives.[^hypo0]
- Epithalamus and related structures (includes pineal gland): circadian and other regulatory roles.[^epi0]

### C. Brainstem
Vital control centers and major conduction pathways:
- Midbrain (mesencephalon)[^mes0]
- Pons
- Medulla (medulla oblongata)

Typical functions:
- breathing and cardiovascular regulation,
- arousal and sleep–wake control,
- reflexes and cranial nerve nuclei,[^cn0]
- routing of ascending (sensory) and descending (motor) tracts.[^tract0]

### D. Cerebellum
Classically: coordination and balance. Modern view: also contributes to:
- timing and fine-tuning of movement,
- prediction and error correction,
- automation of skills,
- some cognitive and affective modulation via connections with cortical systems.[^cb0]

## Cerebral cortex: lobes and key regions

Cortical “lobes” are a convenient map, not rigid functional boxes. Still, they are a useful reference.

### Frontal lobe
Key roles:
- Voluntary motor control and action planning.
- Executive control: planning, inhibition, working memory, goal maintenance.[^exec0]

Important regions:
- Primary motor cortex (M1; precentral gyrus): sends direct motor commands.[^m10]
- Premotor cortex and supplementary motor area (SMA): prepares and sequences actions.[^sma0]
- Prefrontal cortex (PFC): planning, control, working memory, strategy, inhibition.[^pfc0]
- Broca’s area (usually left hemisphere): motor planning of speech production.[^broca0]

### Parietal lobe
Key roles:
- Somatosensation (touch, proprioception) and integration of body/spatial maps.[^soma0]
- Spatial attention and sensorimotor integration.[^att0]

Important regions:
- Primary somatosensory cortex (S1; postcentral gyrus): touch/proprioception.[^s10]
- Posterior parietal areas: spatial reasoning, attention, integration for action.

### Temporal lobe
Key roles:
- Auditory processing, language comprehension, memory systems.

Important regions:
- Primary auditory cortex (A1): basic sound processing.[^a10]
- Wernicke’s area (usually left hemisphere): language comprehension.[^wern0]
- Medial temporal lobe structures (incl. hippocampus): episodic memory formation and context binding.[^hip0]

### Occipital lobe
Key roles:
- Visual processing.

Important regions:
- Primary visual cortex (V1) and adjacent visual areas: early-stage vision.[^v10]

### Insula and cingulate cortex (often treated separately)
These are crucial for “internal state” and control signals:
- Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC): conflict monitoring, error detection, effort/drive allocation.[^acc0]
- Insula: interoception (signals from the body), subjective internal state, integration of visceral sensation with affect.[^ins0]

## Subcortical structures: core regulators

### Thalamus
A major relay and coordination hub.
- Most sensory modalities route through thalamic nuclei to primary sensory cortex (olfaction is a partial exception).[^olf0]
- Thalamus also participates in attention, arousal, and cortico-thalamo-cortical synchronization.[^ctc0]

### Basal ganglia
A set of nuclei strongly involved in action selection, inhibition, habit learning, and reinforcement learning.

Key nodes:
- Striatum (caudate nucleus + putamen): major input gateway.[^bg1]
- Globus pallidus (internal/external segments; GPi/GPe): output regulation.[^gpi0]
- Subthalamic nucleus (STN): “brake” and conflict/inhibition circuitry.[^stn0]
- Substantia nigra (SN): dopaminergic modulation and motor-related control.[^sn_sub0]

Practical summary:
- Basal ganglia do not “move muscles directly.” They bias which action programs are allowed to proceed and which are suppressed, often via loops with cortex and thalamus.[^bg2]

### Limbic-related structures (practical grouping)
“Limbic system” is an older umbrella term; functions are distributed.[^lim0] Still, certain structures recur in affective and memory contexts:

- Amygdala: salience and threat tagging; rapid emotional relevance marking; influences autonomic and hormonal stress responses via hypothalamus and brainstem.[^amy0]
- Hippocampus (medial temporal lobe): episodic memory formation, context and scene binding, consolidation support.[^hip1]
- Hypothalamus: drives, stress axis (HPA), autonomic output control.[^hpa0]

## White matter: major connection systems

White matter tracts enable distributed processing. Key examples:

- Corpus callosum: main bridge between hemispheres.[^cc0]
- Internal capsule: dense conduit for major ascending (sensory) and descending (motor) tracts.[^ic0]
- Arcuate fasciculus: classically associated with language connectivity (e.g., temporal language comprehension to frontal speech production).[^arc0]
- Cingulum bundle: links cingulate and medial cortical regions; relevant to attention, emotion, memory integration (simplified view).[^cing0]

Note: tract naming and functional mapping can be detailed; the above is a practical shortlist.[^tractog0]

## Large-scale brain networks: common operating modes

Many high-level functions are best described as **large-scale networks**—stable patterns of coordination across multiple regions.[^net0] A minimal, practical map:

### Default Mode Network (DMN)[^dmn0]
Typical associations:
- internally oriented thought,
- autobiographical memory,
- self-referential processing,
- narrative and “scene building” (concept-level description).

### Salience Network (SaN)[^san0]
Core idea:
- detects and prioritizes salient internal/external events,[^sal0]
- helps switch between internal processing (DMN-like) and task-control processing (frontoparietal control).

Common nodes: anterior insula and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) (simplified view).

### Frontoparietal Control Network (FPN) / Executive control networks[^fpn0]
Core idea:
- goal maintenance, planning, working memory,
- flexible rule-based control,
- sustained task-oriented processing.

### Attention-related networks (brief)
- Dorsal attention system: top-down attention and goal-directed orienting.[^dan0]
- Ventral attention system: stimulus-driven reorienting (detecting unexpected events).[^van0]

Practical takeaway:
- The same anatomical structures can participate in different network configurations depending on task demands, arousal, and neuromodulatory state.[^net1]

## Information “input”: how signals enter and are processed

General rule (most modalities):
Receptors → peripheral nerves → spinal cord/brainstem → thalamus → primary sensory cortex → associative cortices → integration with memory, attention, goals.

A key idea:
- Early stages extract features.
- Later stages integrate context, meaning, and action relevance.

### Example: vision (simplified pathway)
- Retina → optic nerve → optic chiasm → lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN; thalamus) → primary visual cortex (V1; occipital) → higher visual areas.[^lgn0]
Two broad processing streams (oversimplified but useful):
- “What” stream (ventral, toward temporal): object identity and recognition.[^stream0]
- “Where/how” stream (dorsal, toward parietal): spatial mapping and action guidance.

### Example: hearing (simplified pathway)
- Cochlea → brainstem auditory nuclei → medial geniculate nucleus (MGN; thalamus) → primary auditory cortex (A1; temporal) → higher auditory/language networks.[^mgn0]

### Example: touch and proprioception (simplified pathway)
- Skin/muscle receptors → spinal cord tracts → brainstem relay → thalamus (VPL/VPM nuclei) → S1 (parietal) → integration in parietal/frontal systems.[^vpl0]

### Pain (important nuance)
Pain is not just “where/how strong.” It has:
- Sensory-discriminative component: location/intensity (involves S1/S2 and related pathways).[^s20]
- Affective-motivational component: unpleasantness/urgency/threat (strong involvement of insula, ACC, amygdala-related networks).
Therefore, pain can be “present” physiologically while its conscious salience varies depending on attention and state.

### Smell (exception / special case)
Olfaction has more direct routes to limbic and cortical areas and is less dependent on thalamic relay at early stages compared to other senses. This contributes to its strong linkage with emotion and memory.[^olf1]

## Beyond input→output: predictive processing and its limits

A purely feed-forward “input → processing → output” story is incomplete. The brain also works **top-down**.

### Predictive processing (concept-level framing)
- The brain continuously generates **predictions** about incoming signals.[^pp0]
- Sensory input partially serves to correct these predictions through **prediction errors** (mismatch signals).[^pe0]
- Higher-level context (goals, prior knowledge, emotional state) shapes what is expected and how strongly errors matter.[^priors0]

### Attention as “precision weighting” (simplified idea)
- Attention can be conceptualized as controlling the “gain” on certain signals:
  - increasing the impact of some sensory channels or internal cues,
  - decreasing others.[^prec0]
This interacts strongly with neuromodulators (ACh, NE) and network switching (salience network).[^switch0]

### Why this matters in practice
- Perception is partly **construction**: it is constrained by input but shaped by priors and state.[^construct0]
- Under stress, fatigue, or strong affect, prediction and salience can dominate, changing what is noticed and how it is interpreted.

## Memory as a process: encoding, consolidation, retrieval

Memory is not a single “thing.” It is a set of processes and types.

### The memory cycle (high-level)
1) **Encoding:** forming a memory trace during experience.[^enc0]
2) **Consolidation:** stabilizing and reorganizing traces over time.[^cons0]
3) **Retrieval:** reactivating a trace (which can modify it).[^retr0]

A key practical implication:
- Retrieval is not perfect playback; it is often reconstructive.[^reconstr0]

### Major types (practical taxonomy)
- **Episodic memory:** autobiographical events; strongly linked to hippocampal-medial temporal systems and their cortical interactions.[^epi_mem0]
- **Semantic memory:** facts/knowledge; distributed in cortical systems (simplified framing).[^sem0]
- **Procedural memory:** skills/habits; strongly linked to basal ganglia and cerebellar systems, plus motor cortex.[^proc0]
- **Working memory:** short-term maintenance/manipulation; associated with frontoparietal control networks and prefrontal systems.[^wm0]
- **Emotional memory:** affective associations; amygdala-related modulation of what gets encoded and retained (simplified framing).[^emo0]

### Consolidation (concept-level)
- The hippocampus is often described as a fast-learning “index” that supports binding of episodes and contexts.[^index0]
- Over time, aspects of memory become less dependent on hippocampal indexing and more distributed across cortex (concept-level framing; details are complex).

### Reconsolidation (brief)
- Retrieved memories can become temporarily labile and subject to updating before being “restored,” which partly explains why recall can change memory content.[^recons0]

## Sleep, wakefulness, and levels of consciousness

This section explains why cognition, perception, and memory vary with state.

### Sleep as organized physiology (contrast with general “unconsciousness”)
Sleep is not a shutdown; it is a regulated state with stages:
- **NREM sleep:** ranges from light to deep; associated with specific oscillatory patterns and restorative functions.[^nrem0]
- **REM sleep:** often linked to vivid dreaming; distinct neurochemical and network dynamics.[^rem1]

### Wakefulness and arousal systems
Wakefulness depends strongly on:
- brainstem and hypothalamic arousal systems,
- thalamocortical coordination,[^thalamo0]
- neuromodulators (NE, ACh, histamine, orexin).[^arousal0]

### Why sleep and wake matter for “function”
- Sleep influences memory consolidation and emotional regulation (broadly and variably).[^sleep0]
- Sleep deprivation can degrade:
  - attention stability,
  - working memory,
  - impulse control,
  - affect regulation,
  partly through altered neuromodulatory balance and network coordination.[^sleep1]

### Levels of consciousness (very high-level)
Conscious processing depends on:
- sufficient arousal (global activation),
- and sufficient integration of distributed cortical networks.[^consci0]
Disruptions to these conditions can reduce the stability and accessibility of conscious content.

## Information “output”: how actions and body states are controlled

### A. Somatic motor output (voluntary movement)
Core pathway:
- Motor cortex (M1 and related premotor/SMA planning areas) → corticospinal tract → spinal cord → lower motor neurons → muscles.[^cst0]

Key modulators:
- Basal ganglia: action selection and suppression via cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical loops.[^loop0]
- Cerebellum: timing, coordination, error correction via cerebello-thalamo-cortical loops.[^cb_loop0]

### B. Speech output (as a specialized motor behavior)
- Language comprehension and semantic planning (temporal-parietal networks) → speech planning (frontal language regions, incl. Broca-associated circuitry) → motor execution via motor cortex and brainstem/spinal pathways controlling vocal apparatus.[^speech0]

### C. Autonomic and endocrine output (body state control)
- Hypothalamus and brainstem autonomic centers regulate:
  - sympathetic/parasympathetic outflow (heart rate, blood pressure, digestion),[^aut0]
  - endocrine responses (via pituitary),[^pit0]
  - stress physiology (HPA axis).[^hpa1]
This output is often experienced subjectively as “the body reacting on its own.”

### D. “Internal output”: simulation without movement
Not all “outputs” result in overt action.
- Imagery, inner speech, mental simulation, and planning are forms of neural output that stay within cortical/subcortical loops.[^sim0]
This is relevant when considering how the brain can produce stable internal scenes without external sensory input.

## A note on networks vs “single regions”

A practical rule:
- Most meaningful functions are implemented by networks, not isolated modules.

Examples:
- “Executive control” is a distributed set of frontal and parietal networks with subcortical participation.
- “Threat processing” involves amygdala-tagging plus hypothalamic/brainstem output plus cortical interpretation.
- “Memory” is not only hippocampus; it is hippocampus interacting with multiple cortical systems.

Therefore, when you say “what controls X,” the most accurate answer is usually “which network configuration is dominant right now,” not “which single area.”

## Glossary (short)

- Action potential: an all-or-none electrical spike traveling along a neuron’s axon.[^n1]
- Arousal: level of wakefulness/activation enabling conscious processing.[^m2]
- Awareness: contentful conscious experience; the ability to have and access mental content.[^aware0]
- Encoding: forming a memory trace during experience.[^enc0]
- Consolidation: stabilizing/reorganizing memory traces over time.[^cons0]
- Reconsolidation: updating of a retrieved memory trace before restabilization.[^recons0]
- Thalamic relay: thalamus acting as a hub routing signals to cortex.[^thal0]
- Cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical loop: recurrent circuit linking cortex, basal ganglia (striatal input), thalamus, and back to cortex; supports action selection and control.[^loop0]
- Cerebello-thalamo-cortical loop: cerebellar circuit influencing cortex via thalamus; supports timing and prediction/error correction.[^cb_loop0]
- Default Mode Network (DMN): network associated with internally oriented processing and self-referential cognition (simplified).[^dmn0]
- Salience Network (SaN): network supporting detection of salient events and switching between internal and task-focused modes (simplified).[^san0]
- Frontoparietal control network (FPN): network supporting goal maintenance and flexible executive control (simplified).[^fpn0]
- Interoception: perception of internal bodily signals (heartbeat, breathing, visceral state).[^ins0]
- Neuromodulation: global tuning of circuit behavior by modulators such as dopamine, norepinephrine, serotonin, acetylcholine.[^m0]
- Prediction error: mismatch signal between expected and received input (concept-level framing).[^pe0]
- Salience: perceived importance/priority of a stimulus or internal event.[^sal0]
- NREM/REM: major sleep stage categories with distinct physiology and dynamics.[^nrem0]

[^s1]: A synapse is a junction where one neuron influences another (or a muscle/gland cell).

[^s2]: A neurotransmitter is a chemical messenger released at a synapse that binds to receptors and changes the target cell’s activity.

[^ei1]: E/I balance = excitation/inhibition balance: the relative strength of excitatory vs inhibitory activity in a circuit.

[^ne0]: NE = norepinephrine (noradrenaline). Same molecule, two common naming conventions.

[^ne1]: “Salient events” are events that stand out as important/urgent, either externally (a sudden sound) or internally (a spike of pain).

[^ne2]: “Signal-to-noise” is a ratio-like idea: how strongly meaningful signals stand out relative to background activity.

[^ne3]: “Hyperreactivity” is heightened reactivity to stimuli; it is descriptive here, not a clinical diagnosis.

[^5ht0]: 5-HT = serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine).

[^5ht1]: “Impulsivity” is a tendency to act without sufficient inhibitory control; used descriptively.

[^5ht2]: “Internal state shaping perception” refers to how mood/stress can bias interpretation and attention.

[^5ht3]: “Sleep–wake architecture” means the organization of sleep stages and transitions across the night/day.

[^ach0]: ACh = acetylcholine.

[^ach1]: “Precision” here means how strongly the system treats a channel as reliable; often used in computational framings, but here it is used informally.

[^ach2]: “Internally vs externally driven processing” describes whether activity is dominated by internal thoughts/memory vs sensory-driven demands.

[^rem0]: REM = rapid eye movement; a sleep stage strongly associated with vivid dreaming and distinct neurochemistry.

[^ox0]: Orexin (hypocretin) is a hypothalamic neuropeptide crucial for stabilizing wakefulness; loss leads to narcolepsy in humans.

[^his0]: Histamine is a neuromodulatory system (not just an allergy molecule) contributing to wakefulness and cortical activation.

[^cn0]: Cranial nerve nuclei are brainstem neuron groups that control face/eye movements, swallowing, hearing/balance pathways, etc.

[^cb0]: Cerebellum’s “prediction/error correction” refers to internal models that help refine actions and timing.

[^exec0]: “Executive control” is a broad label for planning, inhibition, working memory, and goal maintenance functions.

[^sma0]: SMA = supplementary motor area, involved in sequencing and internally generated action plans.

[^stn0]: STN = subthalamic nucleus, often conceptualized as part of a “braking”/conflict-control pathway.

[^sn_sub0]: SN here means substantia nigra (a midbrain structure with dopaminergic neurons), not “salience network.”

[^bg2]: “Biasing action programs” means modulating which actions are more likely to be selected given competing options.

[^lim0]: “Limbic system” is historically used, but modern neuroscience treats emotion/motivation/memory as distributed across multiple circuits.

[^amy0]: “Threat tagging” means assigning rapid significance to stimuli, influencing attention and autonomic responses.

[^hip1]: “Consolidation support” here means hippocampal involvement in stabilizing and organizing episodic memory traces.

[^hpa0]: HPA axis = hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal axis, a major hormonal stress system.

[^cc0]: Corpus callosum = the major white-matter bridge between hemispheres.

[^ic0]: Internal capsule = a dense white-matter pathway carrying crucial motor/sensory fibers.

[^arc0]: Arcuate fasciculus = a white-matter tract historically linked to language connectivity between temporal and frontal regions.

[^cing0]: Cingulum bundle = tract linking cingulate and medial cortical areas, relevant to attention/emotion/memory integration.

[^tractog0]: Tractography is an imaging-based method (e.g., diffusion MRI) that estimates white-matter pathways; it has limitations and is not a direct “wiring diagram.”

[^net0]: “Large-scale networks” are recurring patterns of coordinated activity across many regions; their boundaries are not perfectly fixed.

[^dmn0]: DMN = Default Mode Network, associated with internally oriented cognition (a simplified but widely used label).

[^san0]: SaN = Salience Network; abbreviated here as SaN to avoid collision with “SN = substantia nigra.”

[^sal0]: Salience = perceived importance/priority of an event, which can drive attention and action selection.

[^fpn0]: FPN = Frontoparietal Control Network, supporting flexible goal-driven control (simplified label).

[^dan0]: “Dorsal attention” network refers to goal-directed (top-down) attention orienting (simplified).

[^van0]: “Ventral attention” network refers to stimulus-driven reorienting to unexpected events (simplified).

[^net1]: “Network configuration” means which regions are functionally coupled at a given time; this varies with task and state.

[^lgn0]: LGN = lateral geniculate nucleus, a thalamic relay for visual signals.

[^stream0]: Ventral/dorsal “streams” are a classical high-level model; real pathways are more complex, but the distinction is useful.

[^mgn0]: MGN = medial geniculate nucleus, a thalamic relay for auditory signals.

[^vpl0]: VPL/VPM = thalamic nuclei relaying somatosensory information from body (VPL) and face (VPM) to cortex.

[^s20]: S2 = secondary somatosensory cortex; participates in higher-order somatosensory processing (simplified).

[^olf1]: The strong smell–emotion–memory linkage reflects direct/early access to limbic-related circuits (concept-level).

[^pp0]: Predictive processing is a broad family of ideas: perception involves ongoing hypothesis generation, not only feed-forward feature extraction.

[^pe0]: Prediction error = a mismatch signal indicating “what arrived differs from what was expected.”

[^priors0]: “Priors” here means prior expectations formed by experience/context; used informally (not a full Bayesian treatment).

[^prec0]: “Precision weighting” is a concept where the system adjusts how much to trust certain signals; here it is used as an intuition for attentional gain control.

[^switch0]: “Switching” refers to shifts between internal (DMN-like) and task-focused (control) processing, often attributed to salience-related circuitry in simplified accounts.

[^construct0]: “Construction” means perception is constrained by input but shaped by expectations, attention, and state.

[^enc0]: Encoding = forming a memory trace during experience.

[^cons0]: Consolidation = processes that stabilize and reorganize traces over time.

[^retr0]: Retrieval = reactivating stored traces; retrieval can update what is later remembered.

[^reconstr0]: “Reconstructive” recall means memory is not perfect playback; it is rebuilt from partial traces and context.

[^epi_mem0]: Episodic memory = memory for events with contextual details (who/where/when), strongly involving hippocampal–cortical interactions.

[^sem0]: Semantic memory = knowledge of facts and meanings; distributed across cortex (simplified).

[^proc0]: Procedural memory = skills and habits (e.g., motor routines), often linked to basal ganglia/cerebellum and motor cortex.

[^wm0]: Working memory = short-term maintenance/manipulation of information for ongoing tasks, associated with prefrontal/frontoparietal control systems.

[^emo0]: Emotional memory = affective associations; amygdala-related modulation can increase encoding/retention for salient events.

[^index0]: “Index” is an analogy: hippocampus helps bind distributed cortical features into a retrievable episode pointer (concept-level).

[^recons0]: Reconsolidation = the idea that reactivated memories can become temporarily malleable before restabilizing.

[^nrem0]: NREM = non-rapid eye movement sleep; includes lighter and deeper sleep stages with characteristic physiology.

[^rem1]: REM sleep = rapid eye movement sleep; linked to vivid dreaming and distinct neuromodulatory patterns.

[^thalamo0]: Thalamocortical coordination = coordinated activity between thalamus and cortex supporting stable wakeful processing.

[^arousal0]: “Arousal systems” includes multiple neuromodulatory nuclei (e.g., locus coeruleus for NE, basal forebrain for ACh) and hypothalamic systems (orexin).

[^sleep0]: Sleep can support memory consolidation and affect regulation; the magnitude and mechanisms vary by memory type and individual differences.

[^sleep1]: “Sleep deprivation” shifts neuromodulation and network coupling, often reducing prefrontal control and increasing emotional reactivity.

[^consci0]: Conscious access is typically linked (at a high level) to adequate arousal plus sufficient integration across distributed cortical networks.

[^cst0]: Corticospinal tract = major descending pathway from motor cortex to spinal cord controlling voluntary movement; “lower motor neurons” are spinal neurons that directly innervate muscles.

[^loop0]: Cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical loop = recurrent circuit linking cortex, basal ganglia (via striatum), thalamus, back to cortex.

[^cb_loop0]: Cerebello-thalamo-cortical loop = recurrent circuit linking cerebellum, thalamus, cortex; supports timing and error correction.

[^speech0]: “Vocal apparatus” refers to coordinated control of breathing, larynx, tongue, and articulators; it is a complex motor behavior.

[^aut0]: Sympathetic/parasympathetic = two branches of the autonomic nervous system; sympathetic supports mobilization (“fight/flight”), parasympathetic supports rest-and-digest functions.

[^pit0]: Pituitary (hypophysis) = endocrine gland regulated by hypothalamus; releases hormones influencing multiple body systems.

[^hpa1]: HPA axis = hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal axis, coordinating hormonal stress responses (e.g., cortisol in humans).

[^sim0]: “Mental simulation” refers to internally generated representations (imagery, inner speech, planning) that can occur without overt movement.

[^aware0]: Awareness = conscious access to content (what you “experience” and can report); distinct from arousal.
